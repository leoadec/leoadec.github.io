Weak measurements in collision theory

Measurements are a delicate point in quantum mechanics, because small objects are greatly affected by any interaction we have with them, including merely trying to observe their behavior. Weak measurements reduce this interaction to a point where the object is not greatly disturbed by us. Using this, it is possible to get an amplification of values we want to measure, via their weak values.

The standard approach to weak measurements uses a von Neumann interaction to describe how the quantum object evolves in contact with the measurement apparatus. This is a simple model that does not include the kinetic energy of the particles, for example. For this reason, my thesis supervisor, Reginaldo Napolitano, became interested in using the collision theory formalism instead. I and his former student Carlos Brasil finished the calculations and put a paper together after I finished my PhD.

We concluded that collision theory does find the same weak values as the standard approach, given the appropriate set of approximations.

  1. L A Castro, C A Brasil, R d J Napolitano. Weak values in collision theory. Annals of Physics 392, 272-286 (2018). [arXiv:1803.07110]

“Finite-time” measurements

In a real experimental setting, we do not have complete control of the quantum object we are attempting to measure because other processes, generically referred to as noise, are also affecting it. If we are only concerned with the quantum system rather than the precise features of the noise or of the apparatus, this evolution can be calculated using master equations.

My colleague Carlos Brasil, who was also supervised by Reginaldo Napolitano, developed a master equation that described noisy measurements under these terms. This approach was similar to the continuous-measurement formalism, except that the equations did not contain the stochastic back-action term. I helped to calculate this solution with numerical methods and to find other analytical derivations, and together we published three papers about the subject. Also, one of the topics in my PhD thesis was to apply this model to quantum error correction.

Our main observation was that, if the measurement and the noise do not commute, a weak noise is dampened by a strong measurement. This is analogous to the Quantum Zeno Effect, where the constant observation of a decaying atom prevents it from ever decaying.

  1. My PhD thesis (2016). [PDF in Portuguese]
  2. C A Brasil, L A Castro, R d J Napolitano. Efficient finite-time measurements under thermal regimes. European Physical Journal Plus 129, 206 (2014). [arXiv:1309.3621]
  3. C A Brasil, L A Castro, R d J Napolitano. How much time does a measurement take? Foundations of Physics 43, 642-655 (2013). [arXiv:1109.4613]
  4. C A Brasil, L A Castro, R d J Napolitano. Protecting a quantum state from environmental noise by an incompatible finite-time measurement Physical Review A 84, 022112 (2011). [arXiv:1106.4537]

Error correction in short time steps

Overcoming noise and other sources of errors is one of the main challenges to achieve practical quantum computing. There are many proposed strategies to deal with this issue. Quantum error correction is a popular one, and consists of encoding qubits into states of larger sets of qubits, and using the redundancy to correct for any errors that might occur.

When I started my master's degree, my supervisor, Reginaldo Napolitano, had been successfully adapting a different strategy, dynamical decoupling, to a setting that used continuous fields rather than pulses. I attempted something analogous with quantum error correction, seeing how much it could be improved if the syndrome measurement could be done in shortly spaced steps, rather than distantly spaced ones. With some complementary results, I eventually published this a few years after the end of my master's degree.

We saw that, if the errors were quadratic in time, the frequent measurement of the syndrome was capable of reducing the order of magnitude of those errors. This is again analogous to the Quantum Zeno Effect.

  1. L A Castro, R d J Napolitano. Error correction in short time steps during the application of quantum gates. Annals of Physics 367, 199-211 (2016). [arXiv:1109.4796]
  2. My Master's dissertation (2012). [PDF in Portuguese]

Didactic papers

While David Bohm believed that the best way to learn about a new subject is to write a textbook about it, my colleague Carlos Brasil believes the best way was to write a didactic article on the subject. Together, we wrote a couple of articles clarifying some points of measurements in standard quantum mechanics, and calculations in old quantum mechanics.

  1. L A Castro, O P de Sá Neto, C A Brasil. An introduction to quantum measurements with a historical motivation. Accepted for publication on Acta Physica Slovaca
  2. L A Castro, C A Brasil, R d J Napolitano. Elliptical orbits in the phase-space quantization. Revista Brasileira de Ensino de Física 38, e3318. (2016) [arXiv:1605.08027]
  3. C A Brasil, L A Castro. Understanding the pointer states. European Journal of Physics 36, 065024 (2015). [arXiv:1508.04101]
